Family law

When a family changes, difficult questions often arise about the children – where they are going to live, who is to make the important decisions, and how contact is to work. We give you reassuring and clear guidance, with the best interests of the children at the centre.

What we help with

Reliable support in a demanding time

A relationship breakdown or a disagreement between parents can be one of the most stressful periods in life. We know that it is about more than the law – it is about the children and about an everyday life that needs to work. Our task is to give you an overview, sound advice and help to find solutions you can live with.

Most families arrive at arrangements through conversation and cooperation. Where it is difficult to reach agreement, a lawyer can help to clarify rights and obligations, negotiate on your behalf, draw up agreements and, if necessary, present the case before the courts.

  • Assessment of your rights and obligations as a parent
  • Assistance in negotiations and mediation between parents
  • Drafting of agreements on residence and contact
  • Court proceedings when a solution cannot be reached by other means

Questions and answers

Frequently asked questions

Do we have to attend mediation?

In the case of relationship breakdown, separation and parental disputes where you have children together under the age of 16, it is mandatory to attend mediation at the Family Counselling Office (familievernkontoret). As a general rule, the mediation must be completed before a case can potentially be brought before the courts.

What does "the best interests of the child" mean?

The best interests of the child are the decisive consideration in all decisions about residence, parental responsibility and contact. What is best is assessed specifically on the basis of, among other things, the child's age, need for stability, attachment to the parents and the parents' ability to cooperate. There is no single answer that fits all families.

Does the child have the right to be heard?

Yes. Children have the right to be heard in matters concerning themselves, and the child's opinion is to be given weight according to age and maturity. The older the child is, the greater the significance of the child's own views.

What can a lawyer contribute?

A lawyer can help you understand your rights and obligations, assist in negotiations and mediation, draw up agreements and present the case before the courts if it becomes necessary. You are welcome to get in touch for a no-obligation conversation about your situation.
