Victim's advocate

Compensation and assistance after other incidents of violence

Some incidents of violence automatically give the right to a victim's advocate. In other cases, the court makes a discretionary overall assessment. Even when your case does not fit a fixed category, the need for a lawyer may be great – and you may be entitled to compensation.

When the categories do not fit

Have you been subjected to violence?

Certain incidents of violence in principle automatically give the right to a victim's advocate, for example rape. In other cases, a discretionary overall assessment is made, for example of whether there is a significant injury.

There are also incidents of violence that do not fall under any of the other categories within Victim's advocate. One example might be that you have been badly beaten up by a stranger, but the injuries are not significant enough to give the right to a victim's advocate on that basis. Nevertheless, there may be circumstances in your situation that mean you have a great need for legal assistance in the case.

The court can also appoint a victim's advocate where the nature and seriousness of the case warrant it, out of regard for those affected, or where other special circumstances mean a lawyer is needed. The court then makes a discretionary overall assessment of your need for legal help, and several different factors are considered that should be included in the application. We recommend that you contact a lawyer so that all the key elements are included.

Compensation

Compensation claim after violence

If you have been subjected to violence, you may, among other things, be entitled to permanent injury compensation (menerstatning), coverage of loss of income, loss of homemaking capacity, expenses and compensation for non-economic loss (oppreisning).
